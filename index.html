The Physics Explained

The Setup: Two identical ships begin at rest, separated by proper distance D, connected by a rope of natural length D. At t = 0 (simultaneously in the observer frame) both receive identical commands: fire engines with constant proper acceleration α. Their trajectories are identical hyperbolas — just offset by D.

Observer Frame (lab) — why the rope snaps: Both ship centers maintain a constant coordinate gap D for all time. But each moving ship Lorentz-contracts — at speed v the ship has length S₀/γ. An unstressed rope of natural length D would also contract to D/γ. The anchored ships prevent this contraction, forcing the rope to span the full gap D. The rope's proper length grows as γ·D while its natural length stays D → it stretches and eventually snaps.
Proper Frame — the same result, different view: In the instantaneous rest frame of the ships, relativity of simultaneity means the two "start" events are not simultaneous. Ship B (front, in the direction of acceleration) appears to have started its engine slightly earlier. As a result, the proper distance between the ships continuously grows as γ·D. The rope has natural length D and cannot stretch to γ·D indefinitely — it snaps. Both frames agree on the outcome.
Tow Mode contrast — Born rigid motion: If only Ship B fires and drags Ship A through the rope, the situation is completely different. The system moves as a Born rigid body: the proper distance between the ships stays constant at D. In the observer frame the coordinate gap Lorentz-contracts to D/γ as speed increases, but the rope is never stretched beyond its natural length. The rope carries a constant mechanical load (m·α) but experiences zero material strain — it will never snap from relativistic effects.

Frame-independent result: The absolute stretch D·(γ−1) is the same in every frame. Rope attachment point offsets change only L₀ — a longer rope breaks later (lower strain), but the rate of elongation is identical. Bell's paradox is not resolved by clever placement of the rope.
